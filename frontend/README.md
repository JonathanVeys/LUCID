# LUCID

**LUCID turns a plain-English question about global human-trafficking data into a dashboard — generating the charts and the underlying queries automatically.**

## How to use it

Type a question about the trafficking dataset into the box and press enter. LUCID interprets your question, decides how best to visualise the answer, and builds a dashboard of one or more interactive charts. Most questions take 15–30 seconds; complex ones may take a little longer while the system refines its answer. All of this info can be found at the **about** section in the top right of your page.

## What you can ask about

The data covers **reported** trafficking and exploitation incidents drawn from news sources. Each incident includes:

- **Where** it was reported — country and world region
- **What kind** — e.g. sex trafficking, forced labour, scam compounds, debt bondage
- **When** it was reported
- **Who** — victim and perpetrator nationalities, and the number of victims
- **How reliable** the report is — a confidence rating

You can ask about counts, comparisons, trends over time, distributions, and geographic patterns across any of these.

## What it can't answer

The data does **not** contain victim ages or gender, rescue or prosecution outcomes, arrests, financial amounts, named individuals, or trafficking routes. Questions relying on these can't be answered, and LUCID will tell you so rather than guess.

## Example questions to try

- *"Which countries report the most sex trafficking?"* — a world map of incidents by country
- *"Give me an overview of trafficking in Southeast Asia"* — a multi-chart dashboard covering crime types, victims, and trends
- *"How has trafficking reporting changed over time?"* — a trend line over the reporting period
- *"What are the most common crime types?"* — a ranked comparison

## Tips for good questions

- **Broad questions** ("give me an overview of…") produce a multi-chart dashboard; **specific questions** ("how many incidents in Vietnam?") produce a single focused chart.
- Name what you want to see — a place, a crime type, a time period — to get a sharper result.
- Remember the data reflects what was **reported in the news**, not the true total number of incidents.
