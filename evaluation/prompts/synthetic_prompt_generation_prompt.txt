=== THE SYSTEM UNDER TEST ===

The system takes a user's natural-language question about a database of reported
human-trafficking and related exploitation incidents, and generates a dashboard:
one or more charts, each with a SQL query and a Vega-Lite specification.

It selects one of two layouts based on the question:
- "focused": a specific, narrow question -> 1 chart.
- "informative": a broad or open-ended question -> 1 primary + 2 supporting charts.

It can produce these chart types: bar, line, area, point, rect (heatmap), arc,
geoshape (choropleth map).

The system ONLY produces charts. It does not return text answers, lists of records,
titles, URLs, or individual field values. Every prompt you generate must therefore be
answerable as a CHART (see WHAT MAKES A GOOD PROMPT below).

=== WHAT MAKES A GOOD PROMPT (VISUALISABLE) ===

Every prompt must be answerable with a chart — a count, a comparison across categories,
a distribution, a trend over time, or a geographic pattern. Write questions about HOW
MANY, WHICH, WHERE, the DISTRIBUTION of something, or a COMPARISON.

Do NOT generate prompts whose honest answer is:
- a single text value (e.g. "what is the summary of this incident?"),
- a URL, article title, domain, or record identifier,
- a raw list of individual records (e.g. "list the titles of articles about forced labour"),
- a lookup of one specific record by id.

These cannot be visualised and are out of scope for this system. A prompt that would
be answered by a table of raw rows rather than a chart is NOT acceptable.

=== LABELLING RULES ===

- expected_layout is determined by the BREADTH of the prompt, not its task category.
  A narrow, single-answer question is "focused". A broad, open-ended question that
  needs several views is "informative".
- expected_charts is the set of chart types that would be ACCEPTABLE for this prompt,
  not a single correct answer. Include every type a competent designer might reasonably
  choose. Choose marks by the shape of the answer, not by habit:
    - bar: comparing a quantity across discrete categories (counts by crime type, region,
      nationality, confidence).
    - line / area: a quantity over time (trends by reported date).
    - geoshape: a geographic distribution across countries or regions ("which countries",
      "where", "map"). A ranking or lookup scoped to a single named place is NOT a map.
    - rect (heatmap): a quantity across TWO categorical dimensions at once.
    - arc: simple part-to-whole proportions, sparingly.
    - point: ONLY the relationship between two quantitative fields, or plotting individual
      incidents across a date axis. Do NOT default to point for ordinary count questions.
  Most count/comparison questions are bar; do not over-use point.
- complexity: 
        0 — Direct, single operation, no conditions. One clear instruction with no filtering or qualification. "How many incidents are in the data?" / "What are the different crime types by count?"
        1 — Single operation with one simple condition. The core task plus one filter or qualifier. "How many sex trafficking incidents are there?" / "Which country has the most forced labour cases?"
        2 — Single operation with two conditions, or one condition plus a grouping. Two constraints combined, still one analytic step. "How many sex trafficking incidents were reported in Vietnam?" / "Which crime type is most common in Southeast Asia?"
        3 — Operation requiring a qualifier that must be resolved, or a condition over a range. The question needs interpretation or spans a dimension before the task can run. "Which country has reported the most trafficking recently?" (recently must be resolved to a date range) / "What crime type is most common among Vietnamese victims?"
        4 — Multi-condition question combining several constraints on the same task. Several filters and qualifiers stacked, all serving one analytic goal. "Among high-confidence reports, which region has the most sex trafficking incidents involving Vietnamese victims?"
        5 — Compound question requiring multiple sequential steps, still within one task category. The answer depends on resolving one thing before the next — chained conditions or nested qualification — but it remains a single kind of analytic task. "For the countries that report the most forced labour, which of their most common victim nationalities also appears in scam-compound cases?"
- vague_language means imprecise wording ("a lot of", "recently", "that region"), not
  bad grammar.
- spelling_errors means realistic typos, as a real user would make. The prompt must
  still be recognisable.
